# Museums

## Where Lumina's art lives

All seven came from the **Art Institute of Chicago**: the El Greco and the Inness, a Dupré
drawing, a Munch etching, an impression of Hokusai's wave, and the rest. The museum put them
online for anyone to use, free and forever. If you go to one place because of this project, go
there. The Inness moonrise in person is worth the flight.

Two of the seven are by **Edvard Munch**. For him, the **MUNCH museum in Oslo** is a pilgrimage.
A whole building of one restless mind.

## Museums that give their art away

This is the part worth knowing. A growing number of museums have released their public-domain
collections under open licenses, in high resolution, free to download and reuse. They are the
reason Lumina could exist, and the reason you can rebuild it with your own taste. Pull a few
images, drop them in `art/`, and you have a different screensaver in ten minutes.

The good ones:

- **Art Institute of Chicago** — clean public API, thousands of public-domain works. Start here.
- **The Metropolitan Museum of Art**, New York — Open Access. Around half a million images under CC0.
- **Rijksmuseum**, Amsterdam — Rijksstudio. Vermeer and Rembrandt at absurd resolution.
- **Cleveland Museum of Art** — Open Access with an API, quietly one of the best.
- **The Getty**, Los Angeles — Open Content Program.
- **Smithsonian** — Open Access. Millions of items, art and otherwise.
- **National Gallery of Art**, Washington — open images, no strings.
- **Paris Musées** — the city of Paris collections, open and high-res.

Search any of them for "public domain," sort by what stops you, and download the largest file
they offer.

## If you can only see a few in person

Not a ranking, just a short list I would not skip:

- The **Art Institute of Chicago**, for the reasons above and the Caillebotte rainy street.
- The **Musée d'Orsay**, Paris, an old train station full of light.
- The **Frick** or the **Met**, New York, opposite moods, both essential.
- The **Rijksmuseum**, Amsterdam, for the Night Watch and the rooms that lead to it.
- The **Prado**, Madrid, for the Velázquez and the dark Goyas in the basement.

Stand close enough to see the brushwork. Then stand back until it becomes weather again. That
second step is the whole trick, and it is the same trick Lumina is doing with your data.
